Most air purifier marketing is noise. "99.97% of particles" sounds definitive but tells you nothing about how fast a purifier clears a room, how quiet it is at the fan speeds where it actually cleans, or whether its CADR ratings translate to real-world performance in the room sizes it claims. We cut through this by testing with a calibrated particulate meter — measuring PM2.5 and PM10 counts before and at 30-minute intervals after running each purifier in a sealed 250 sq ft room with a controlled particle source.

The filter type distinction matters first: true HEPA (removes 99.97% of particles ≥0.3 microns) is different from "HEPA-type" or "HEPA-like" filters that don't meet the standard. Every purifier worth recommending uses a true HEPA filter — it's not a premium feature, it's the baseline.

CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate) is a more useful number than coverage area. CADR measures how quickly a purifier delivers clean air in cubic feet per minute (cfm), separately for smoke, dust, and pollen. Divide CADR by the room's square footage and multiply by ceiling height to estimate air changes per hour — you want at least 4–5 for meaningful air quality improvement.

## Filter Replacement

HEPA filters have a finite lifespan — typically 6–12 months depending on air quality, run time, and filter size. Running a purifier in a dusty or smoky environment accelerates loading. Most models have a filter replacement indicator; trust it over the clock.

**Don't wash your HEPA filter.** Water damages the fiber arrangement and destroys its efficiency. Pre-filters (the outer mesh layer that catches large particles) are often washable and should be cleaned monthly — this extends the HEPA filter's life significantly by reducing the load it handles.

Carbon filters handle gases and odors: cooking smells, VOCs, pet odors, smoke. The Winix 5500-2's washable AOC carbon filter is an exception to the replacement cycle — rinse it periodically and it regenerates. Standard carbon filters last 3–6 months and are typically sold as a unit with the HEPA filter. Factor annual filter cost into your purchase decision; a cheaper purifier with expensive filters can cost more over three years than a pricier purifier with affordable consumables.

## FAQ

**Is a HEPA purifier or an ionizer safer?**
True HEPA filtration is the recommended approach by most air quality experts. Ionizers (which electrically charge particles to make them stick to surfaces) can produce trace amounts of ozone as a byproduct — a known lung irritant. Many purifiers with ionizers (including the Coway and Winix) allow you to disable the ionizer function while keeping HEPA filtration active. We recommend running HEPA without ionization for daily use.

**Can an air purifier help with wildfire smoke?**
Yes — with caveats. A True HEPA purifier handles PM2.5 particles (the fine particulate matter in smoke) effectively. Run it on high during smoke events, keep windows and doors closed, and consider a unit with a robust carbon filter since smoke also contains VOCs and gases that HEPA alone doesn't address. During severe air quality events, indoor air quality with a running purifier is significantly better than outdoor air.

**Where should I place my air purifier?**
Position it in the room where you spend the most time, with at least 6–12 inches of clearance on all sides for airflow. The center of the room is ideal if practical; against a wall works if the vents aren't blocked. Don't put it in a corner — the airflow circulation is reduced. For bedrooms, place it a few feet from the bed, not directly blowing at your face.

**Does an air purifier remove VOCs?**
HEPA filtration alone doesn't remove VOCs (volatile organic compounds) — these are gases, not particles, and pass through the fiber membrane. Carbon filtration handles VOCs. All three purifiers here include a carbon filter alongside HEPA. For very high VOC environments (new furniture off-gassing, paint, solvents), a purifier with a thick activated carbon bed is more effective than one with a thin carbon layer.
