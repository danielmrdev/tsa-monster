## Ingredients Worth Knowing

**Niacinamide (Vitamin B3):** One of the most thoroughly studied topical actives. At 4–10% concentration, peer-reviewed evidence supports improvements in pore appearance, uneven skin tone, and sebum production. Fragrance-free formulations are important — it's a commonly sensitizing ingredient when combined with fragrances or essential oils. Paula's Choice delivers 10% in a simple, stable formula.

**Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid):** The antioxidant with the strongest evidence base for photodamage prevention and brightening. L-ascorbic acid is the bioavailable form — ascorbyl glucoside and other esters are less well-supported. Stability is the challenge: L-ascorbic acid oxidizes rapidly when exposed to light or air, turning yellow-orange and losing efficacy. The SkinCeuticals formulation (patent: specific pH + concentration + ferulic acid combination) has the most robust clinical evidence for photoprotection enhancement.

**Hyaluronic acid:** A humectant — it draws moisture from the environment into the skin, not from deeper layers. Multi-weight formulations (different molecular sizes) address both surface hydration (large molecules) and slightly deeper moisture retention (smaller molecules). It's a supportive ingredient rather than a transformative one; its value is maintaining the skin's hydration state to support barrier function.

**Retinol/Retinoids:** Not represented here because they warrant their own guide. The evidence base is the strongest of any topical active for anti-aging and acne. They're also the most likely to cause irritation and require an adaptation period. Begin low (0.025–0.1%), use at night only, and add SPF to your morning routine — retinoids increase photosensitivity.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between a serum and a moisturizer?**
Serums are concentrated active delivery vehicles — typically water-based or oil-based, with higher percentages of active ingredients, designed to penetrate before a moisturizer. A moisturizer's job is to seal in what's beneath it and support the skin barrier. You apply serum first, let it absorb, then apply moisturizer on top. A serum doesn't replace a moisturizer; they do different jobs.

**How do I know if my vitamin C serum has oxidized?**
L-ascorbic acid serums are typically colorless or very pale yellow when fresh. Oxidation turns them yellow, then orange, then brown. Oxidized vitamin C has lost its antioxidant activity and may cause mild irritation. Buy from brands that package vitamin C in opaque, air-limiting bottles, store it in the refrigerator, and replace it every 3–6 months. The SkinCeuticals C E Ferulic comes in an amber bottle and maintains stability well when stored correctly.

**What order do I apply serums in?**
Thinnest to thickest is the standard rule: micellar water or toner first, then water-based serums (e.g., hyaluronic acid), then thicker serums (e.g., niacinamide boosters), then moisturizer, then SPF (morning) or facial oil (evening). For vitamin C, apply after cleansing in the morning before anything else — it works as a photoprotection enhancer alongside (not instead of) SPF.

**Can I use retinol and vitamin C together?**
Use them at different times of day rather than layering them together. Vitamin C is a morning ingredient (antioxidant protection during sun exposure). Retinol is a night ingredient (works while you sleep, photosensitizing). If you use both products, the timing separation prevents irritation and maintains both actives at their optimal pH.
