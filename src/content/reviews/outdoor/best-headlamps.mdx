Headlamps get taken for granted until they don't work. A dead battery at 10pm on a dark trail, a control interface that requires three button presses to cycle from blinding full-brightness back to camp mode, a beam that illuminates a perfect circle directly in front of you but nothing in your peripheral field — these are the details that separate a good headlamp from a frustrating one. We ran six models through a battery of tests: night hikes on uneven terrain, camp chores requiring close-distance work, tent reading sessions, and sustained runtime testing until cutoff.

The surprise finding: raw lumen count is the least useful spec for most hikers. What matters is beam pattern (how the light spreads), mode usability (can you navigate modes without removing gloves), battery runtime at real-use settings (not max brightness), and cold-weather performance (battery chemistry degrades fast below freezing).

These three covered the spectrum from serious backcountry use to ultralight trail running.

## Battery Life in Practice

Manufacturer battery life claims are tested at lower brightness settings than you'll actually use on the trail. At full brightness (400 lumens), expect roughly 2 hours from a full charge on the Black Diamond Spot 400-R. At the medium setting you'll realistically use for hiking — around 100–150 lumens — expect 6–8 hours. The Petzl Actik Core's hybrid battery system is its biggest practical advantage: if you drain the internal battery, three AAA cells get you home.

Cold weather is the silent battery killer. Lithium-ion capacity drops roughly 20–30% at 32°F and continues falling below that. For winter camping or alpine starts, consider a headlamp with AAA compatibility (lithium disposables maintain capacity in cold), or keep your rechargeable headlamp in an inside pocket and rotate it to your head as needed.

## FAQ

**Should I choose rechargeable or AAA batteries?**
For most trail use, rechargeable wins: lower cost per use, consistent brightness, USB charging. The exception is winter camping and remote expeditions where charging isn't possible. In those contexts, AAA lithium batteries (not alkaline) maintain performance in cold and are available at any outfitter. The Petzl Actik Core's hybrid design gives you both options.

**When do I actually need red light mode?**
Red light preserves night vision — your eyes adapt to darkness over 20–30 minutes, and white light resets that adaptation instantly. Use red mode for camp tasks when you want to preserve your ability to see the surrounding terrain, for reading in a tent without disturbing your partner, and for any situation where you need to look at something close without blinding yourself or others. Most headlamps bury red mode behind multiple button presses; the Black Diamond Spot 400-R puts it on a dedicated click.

**How do I protect my headlamp in cold weather?**
Keep it warm until you need it. Store it inside your sleeping bag or jacket overnight, and keep it in an inner pocket during cold-weather hiking rather than on your head or in your pack's outer pocket. When you do need it, it will perform at full capacity for the first few minutes — which is usually enough to get camp sorted.

**What does IPX waterproofing actually mean?**
IPX ratings describe water resistance, not waterproofing. IPX4 (Petzl Actik Core) resists water splashing from any direction. IPX6 resists powerful water jets. IPX8 (Black Diamond Spot 400-R) can be submerged up to 1.1 meters for 30 minutes. For most hiking use, IPX4 is adequate. For serious rain or creek crossings, IPX8 provides meaningful extra protection.
