A mattress topper is not a substitute for a good mattress. But for a mattress that's slightly too firm, starting to sag at the edges, or simply not the right feel for your sleep position, the right topper can meaningfully improve sleep quality at a fraction of the cost of a replacement. It's also the right tool for a guest bed that needs to be ready for a wide range of sleepers without investing in a customized mattress.

We tested eight toppers over four weeks each on the same base mattress — a medium-firm innerspring that produced consistent pressure mapping results. We used a body pressure mapping pad to assess where pressure concentrated across different sleep positions (back, side, stomach). We tracked temperature across the night using contact thermometers in the foam surface and sleep diary entries about sleep quality, temperature comfort, and morning soreness. We also assessed durability over the four-week period: how much loft was retained, whether the foam or fill shifted, and how the cover held up to washing.

Four toppers earned a recommendation. The others either compressed too quickly, trapped too much heat, or failed to meaningfully improve pressure distribution over the base mattress.

## Choosing the Right Type

**Memory foam** is the most common topper material. It contours to your body shape, which relieves pressure points (particularly hips and shoulders for side sleepers) but creates a "stuck" sensation that some sleepers dislike. Memory foam also retains heat — gel and graphite infusions partially address this, but no memory foam topper is as cool as fiber or latex.

**Fiber/down alternative** toppers feel dramatically different: soft, light, and plush rather than contouring. They work best for sleepers who want more cushion rather than pressure relief, and for sleepers who run hot — fiber doesn't retain heat the way foam does. They compress over time and eventually need fluffing or replacement.

**Latex** toppers (not represented in our top picks this cycle, though we tested two) offer responsive contouring without the heat-retention issues of memory foam. They're also notably heavier, more expensive, and have a distinct "springy" feel that suits some sleepers perfectly and bothers others. If memory foam's cradling feel is uncomfortable, latex is worth investigating.

**Thickness.** A 2-inch topper makes a modest adjustment to your mattress feel. A 3-inch topper makes a significant change. A 4-inch topper is essentially adding a new sleeping surface — your base mattress becomes more support structure than sleep surface. For anyone with a mattress that needs more than minor correction, 3–4 inches is necessary to feel the difference.

## How We Tested

Each topper was tested on a queen-size Saatva Classic in medium-firm as the base mattress. The base mattress remained constant across all toppers. We used a TexTest pressure mapping pad to assess peak pressure under the hips in side-lying position (the most common pressure pain point). Temperature measurements were taken with a surface contact thermometer at a mid-sleep equivalent of 4 hours in a room held at 68°F. Durability was assessed by measuring topper height before and after the four-week test period, and by washing and drying once per manufacturer instructions to assess fiber and seam durability.

## FAQ

**Will a mattress topper fix a sagging mattress?**
A topper can mask minor sagging (under 1.5 inches) by providing a fresh surface that spans the dip. For significant sagging, however, a topper will conform to the sag and provide little improvement. The support structure of a mattress is in the coils or base foam — a surface layer can't compensate for failed support underneath. If your mattress sags more than 1.5–2 inches, a topper is a short-term fix at best.

**What mattress topper is best for back pain?**
This depends on your sleep position and the source of the pain. Side sleepers with hip or shoulder pain typically benefit from a medium-soft memory foam topper that relieves pressure at those contact points. Back sleepers with lower back pain often need to maintain spinal alignment, which means a topper that's too soft can worsen the problem — a medium-firmness foam at 2–3 inches tends to work better. If pain is severe or persistent, consult a sleep specialist before buying.

**Can you wash a mattress topper?**
Fiber and down alternative toppers can typically be machine-washed (check the specific tag — most require a large-capacity machine). Memory foam toppers cannot be machine-washed — the agitation shreds the foam. Spot clean foam toppers with a damp cloth and mild soap. Air-dry thoroughly before re-covering. Most toppers come with a washable cover separate from the topper itself; washing the cover is sufficient for routine maintenance.

**How long does a mattress topper last?**
Memory foam toppers: 3–5 years before compression becomes noticeable. Fiber/down alternative: 1–3 years before significant loft loss. Latex: 5–7 years. Durability is closely tied to price — budget foam compresses faster. Rotating the topper 180 degrees every few months extends life by distributing wear.
