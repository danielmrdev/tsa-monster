Trekking poles are one of those pieces of gear that feels optional until you've used good ones on a steep descent with a heavy pack. A proper pair reduces knee stress on downhills by an estimated 25%, improves stability on loose terrain, and gives you something to plant when scrambling. The research on their benefits — particularly for knee preservation on long routes — is consistent enough that most serious hikers don't go without them.

But poles also vary enormously in quality, weight, grip comfort, and locking mechanism reliability. We spent several months hiking with eight pairs across flat trail, rocky scrambles, alpine terrain, and creek crossings. We hiked enough miles with each to assess grip comfort over extended periods, shake-tested the locks after repeated adjustments, and evaluated pole tip durability on granite and sandstone. We also assessed packability — increasingly relevant as ultralight and fastpacking approaches make pole stowage in running vests and packs a real design consideration.

Four pairs rose clearly above the rest.

## Key Decisions Before You Buy

**Aluminum vs. carbon fiber.** Aluminum poles are heavier but more durable under impact — they bend rather than crack when you fall on them or lever them between rocks. Carbon fiber poles are lighter but can crack catastrophically under lateral stress. For casual hiking on maintained trails, carbon's weight savings are worth considering. For technical terrain where you're scrambling and planting hard, aluminum's durability is worth the weight penalty.

**Adjustable vs. fixed length.** Adjustable poles let you change length on the fly — shorter on steep climbs, longer on descents. Fixed-length poles eliminate the locking mechanism (a potential failure point) and save weight. Trail runners and ultralight hikers often prefer fixed-length for simplicity; most other hikers prefer adjustability.

**Locking mechanisms.** Twist-lock (screwing the sections to lock) is simple and cheap but harder to adjust with cold hands. Flick-lock (lever mechanism) is faster and easier but adds a small amount of weight. Leki's SpeedLock 2 is the most reliable lever mechanism we tested — it held under significant lateral pressure throughout testing without any loosening.

**Grip material.** Cork grips wick moisture, mold slightly to your hand over time, and stay comfortable across a wide temperature range. Foam grips are lighter and comfortable but can absorb sweat and degrade over seasons of use. Rubber grips are used on winter poles — they insulate from cold metal but get clammy in warm weather.

**Pole tip baskets.** Standard poles come with small summer baskets. If you use poles in snow or mud, fit large baskets. Most poles accept interchangeable baskets. Don't hike deep mud without them — tips sink and grip is lost.

## How We Tested

Each pair was tested on a minimum of 30 miles of hiking with a 25–30 lb pack. We hiked the same 8-mile loop route with each pair for consistency on the primary terrain test (mixed rocky and dirt trail with 1,800 ft elevation gain and loss). We also tested each pair on extended flat terrain, creek crossings, and a technical scramble section requiring pole planting on granite. Lock adjustment was tested by making 10 full length adjustments per pole per test session and assessing any drift. Grip comfort was rated after a 6-hour continuous day hike.

## FAQ

**What length should trekking poles be?**
A general starting point: when you hold the grip with the tip on the ground, your elbow should be at 90 degrees. For most adults this puts the pole length between 100–130 cm. On steep ascents, shorten by 5–10 cm; on descents, lengthen by 5–10 cm. Many hikers just find a comfortable all-day length and leave it fixed.

**Do trekking poles actually help knees?**
Yes, and this is well-documented. On downhill terrain with a loaded pack, poles shift some of the impact load to your upper body, measurably reducing force on knee joints. The benefit is most significant on steep descents and when carrying heavy loads. For hikers with existing knee issues, poles are often essential.

**Can I fly with trekking poles?**
Standard trekking poles must be checked — they're prohibited in carry-on luggage due to pointed tips. Folding poles like the Leki Micro Vario or Black Diamond Distance Z pack down small enough to fit in checked luggage without a bag fee at most carriers.

**How do I replace worn pole tips?**
Carbide tips are wear items — plan to replace them every 200–400 miles depending on terrain. Replacement tips are inexpensive (usually under $10 for a set) and thread on by hand. Most poles from reputable brands use standard thread sizes and accept tips from multiple manufacturers.

The Black Diamond Trail Ergo Cork is our top recommendation for most hikers.
